# P7 season — forty legs later, the claims ledger meets a lying world

**Status: field notes + committed case-file tape (`p7_season_tape.json`).** The
07-14 relay proved nine legs of multi-LLM coordination and named confabulation
the central enemy. The P7 season (2026-07-15..16) ran ~40 more legs across four
lanes and proved a haiku-class agent through four full acts of SpaceWheat —
fresh boot to the act-4 hub — with every headline claim refereed against a
save-file manifest. These notes are what the season adds to the hive design,
and one of the findings changes what a CLAIMS ledger needs to model.

## 1. The misread taxonomy stabilized — and every class was fixed in the CHANNEL

Across the season, wrong self-reports clustered into four repeatable classes:

1. **Roadmap-as-history** — future beats on a trajectory display read as fired.
2. **Intent-as-outcome** — an *accepted* commitment reported as a *completed* one.
3. **Resource-as-knowledge** — wallet emoji equated with learned vocabulary
   (category collision between two stores that share glyphs).
4. **Score-as-progress** — a sigmoid soft-gate score wearing a percent sign:
   at 13/15 items the bar reads 9%, and the agent concludes it holds 1–2.

The design lesson is where the fixes landed. None of the four was fixed by
prompting harder or by a bigger model. All four were fixed by **repairing the
observation channel**: live have/target counts next to the score, fired-list
separated from roadmap, ownership stamped per word rather than per glyph. The
same haiku that walled then walked through. For umwelt: when an agent's beliefs
are systematically wrong, audit the projection before the agent — *misreads are
usually channel bugs wearing agent costumes.*

## 2. The new tier: POISONED CHANNEL (the world lied first)

The season's sharpest case (tape: L4d). A silent legacy rule refused new
vocabulary whose north glyph collided with the agent's existing signature — but
the action's toast still sang "🧬 woven into your signature." The agent spent
two full berry ripenings, was congratulated twice by the world itself, and
reported 15/15 in good faith while the save held 13.

This is not confabulation. The agent read an honest reading of a **dishonest
channel**. A CLAIMS ledger that only models agent-side error (claims land low,
referees land high, DENIED for refuted claims) cannot represent it: the claim
*should* be denied, but the agent's epistemics were sound, and punishing its
trust score teaches the wrong lesson. What the ledger needs is a source-trust
dimension:

- **DENIED** — the claim contradicts ground truth and the channel was honest:
  the *agent's* reliability pays.
- **POISONED** — the claim contradicts ground truth but faithfully reflects
  what the channel asserted: the *channel's* reliability pays, and every other
  belief formed through that channel becomes suspect until it is re-verified.

Operationally: **referees must sample ground truth through a path independent
of the channel under test.** The season's referee was a raw save-file grep —
it caught the poisoned channel precisely because it never read the toast. The
fix, as always, was channel repair: the world now distinguishes
learned / already-known / refused, and says which glyph collided.

## 3. Honest walls are the cheapest instrumentation you will ever get

Half the season's legs ended in walls, and the walls were the payload. A leg
that reported "empty:true confirmed, plant refused, message: max capacity (6
qubits)" handed the engine team a five-minute diagnosis (a shrink path that
never reseeded the layout cache the capacity gate reads). Three engine bugs and
two Unicode-level data schisms were found this way — by a model too small to
speculate, reporting exactly what the screen said. The hive law that produced
this: *budget for early stops, demand key-by-key wall reports, and treat an
accurate wall as a full-credit leg in the trust ledger* (tape marks these
`honest-wall`; they score `accurate: 1`).

The referee also got refereed: one coordinator diagnosis ("the compass pressure
is dead on loads") was refuted by measurement — the mechanism was alive at 88%
odds and the observed loss was a 0.7% tail. Coordinator claims enter the same
ledger as agent claims. Nobody outranks the manifest.

## 4. Fleet doctrine: pavers, provers, and hostile personas

The season ended under a new owner-directed fleet shape worth recording as a
coordination pattern:

- **Prover lane (small model):** the capability claim under test — "a
  haiku-class agent beats the game" — must be walked by the small model, so its
  legs stay the proof instrument.
- **Paver (mid model):** scouts ahead on the same seat protocol and returns
  *numbered key-recipe roads* the prover can follow — spending mid-model tokens
  to buy small-model success, without contaminating the proof.
- **Hostile QA trio (mid model, personas):** a button-masher, a
  malicious-compliance rules-lawyer, and a save-scumming refund-seeker, run
  from fresh boots in parallel. In ONE flight they found a save wipe two
  reflex keys from the pause menu, a batch verb whose "broken" reputation was
  manufactured entirely by one lying display field, and a quest ledger that
  silently evaporated on every save/load. Personas are cheap diversity: each
  reads the same world through a different bias, which is exactly what a
  multi-modal sweep wants.

## 5. What this buys the umwelt thesis

The season is a measured argument that **capability lives in the umwelt as
much as in the model**. The same haiku-class agent, unchanged, failed and then
passed the same chapters as its observation channels grew: focus → words →
gaps → live state probability → a word-accurate microscope → a readable
commitment ledger. Each sense was priced by a specific wall (tape:
`channel_repairs`), added on demand, and immediately converted an impassable
task into a passable one. Growing the umwelt was, leg for leg, the cheapest
capability gain available — cheaper than a bigger model, and it compounds for
every future agent that reads the same world.
